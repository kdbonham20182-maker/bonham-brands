The Bonham Conclusion

We chose Clear Creek Pinto Beans by Palouse Brand because they offer something rare in the dried bean category — complete farm-to-consumer traceability combined with Glyphosate Residue Free certification from The Detox Project.

Clear Creek is grown on a 125-year-old family farm in Washington's Palouse region — some of the most fertile dryland farming soil in North America. Claims & Certifications

What Clear Creek Claims — And What Each One Means

Certified Glyphosate Residue Free — The Detox Project
The Detox Project is an independent ISO-accredited laboratory program. This certification means the product was independently lab-tested and confirmed to have no detectable glyphosate or its metabolite AMPA at the lowest government-recognized detection limits. For beans, this is the most critical certification available — because most conventional beans are sprayed directly with glyphosate as a pre-harvest desiccant. Publicly listed at detoxproject.org.
Non-GMO Verified
Pinto beans are not currently a commercially available GMO crop, but Palouse Brand's explicit Non-GMO verification confirms this directly across their entire product line.
Non-Irradiated
Irradiation is commonly used to extend shelf life on imported legumes. Non-irradiated means the product has not been exposed to ionizing radiation as a preservation method. Because these beans are non-irradiated, they can still sprout — a sign of a live, unaltered product.
Desiccant-Free
No desiccant packets. No chemical moisture-control agents. Just beans.
Triple-Cleaned in HACCP-Certified Facility
HACCP — Hazard Analysis and Critical Control Points — is the food safety management system used by the FDA. Triple-cleaning means three separate cleaning passes to remove debris, dust, and foreign material before packaging. Processed in an FDA-approved, food-safe facility.
Vertically Integrated — Field Traceable
Palouse Brand grows, harvests, processes, packages, and ships their own products from their own farm. Field location, harvest dates, and photos are available to customers. No supply chain opacity. No third-party sourcing. The same family that farmed this land 125 years ago is farming it today.

The Broader Picture

Why Glyphosate in Beans Is a Bigger Problem Than You Think

Conventional bean farming uses glyphosate not just as a weed killer during the growing season — it uses it as a harvest tool. Approximately 7 to 14 days before harvest, farmers spray glyphosate directly on the standing bean crop to kill the plants uniformly, accelerating drying and making mechanical harvesting faster. The result is that glyphosate is applied at near-maximum concentration directly to the food product in the final days before it is harvested and sold.

This is not a residue from early-season soil treatment. This is the active ingredient in Roundup applied directly to the bean you are eating. Independent testing by The Detox Project and Mamavation has confirmed detectable glyphosate in a significant proportion of conventional dried beans sold in the United States.

Glyphosate Residue Free certification from The Detox Project independently verifies this is not present in Clear Creek beans at the lowest government-recognized detection limits.
